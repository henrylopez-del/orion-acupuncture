One Practitioner
— A short bio

Why this work.

A background in nursing before all of it. Five years on a respiratory ward in Galway, then a midwifery rotation, then the realisation that the patients who got better were the ones who had been listened to. So I went to Beijing for the rest of the listening.

Dr. Wang Ju-Yi was teaching channel palpation when I arrived, which is the practice of reading the body's meridians with the hands before the needles. That changed how I work. Most of the diagnosis happens in the first ten minutes of the session, with no instruments at all.

What I treat most

Stress-driven sleep loss is the largest group. Hormonal regulation across the cycle is the second. Fertility support, including alongside IVF protocols, is a third I take a lot of care with. Post-illness recovery, especially after long viral fatigue, has grown since 2022.

I keep my caseload deliberately narrow so I can see real progress with each patient. If what you're bringing isn't a fit, I'll say so on the first call and refer you to someone better placed.

How I think about treatment

Conservatively. The body knows what to do, the needles remind it. I'd rather do less and see how it responds than throw the whole toolkit at one session. Most patients see meaningful change within four sessions. If we're not seeing it by six, we change the plan or refer out.

I write a printed protocol after every session. Foods to add, foods to skip, one or two simple rituals for the week. It is not a list of supplements. The work happens between treatments, not on the table.

Continuing study

I sit a mentorship with Lonny Jarrett twice a year on classical Chinese medicine, and a yearly intensive in Beijing on channel palpation. The clinic closes for two weeks each January for this.

Outside the studio

Sea swimming at the Forty Foot, slowly improving cooking, two dogs called Saoirse and Brendan. Reading, mostly fiction. The studio is closed on Sundays and Mondays for a reason.
